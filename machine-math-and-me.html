Machine, Math, and Me

I have recently gotten interesting in teaching math to computers. This is different from doing computations as here we are trying to teach math to computers the same way we understand it.

The software I use for this is Lean. Lean is an open-source software that understands mathematics and can check correctness of proofs. It was developed my computer scientists at Microsoft Research but has since been overtaken my mathematicians who now form the leanprover-community and maintain the rapidly growing mathlib library.

My primary interest in Lean is as a potential tool for teaching math proofs. When it comes to learning to write proofs, the biggest hurdle is the lack of immediate feedback. Even well meaning students often find it difficult to spot logical errors in their proofs. I strongly believe that having a software that can pinpoint out errors in proofs will effectively solve this problem.

My secondary interest lies in combining machine learning and theorem proving. Some progress is already being made on this front for a more basic theorem prover called Metamath.

These are very long term goals as the field of theorem proving, although rapidly growing, is still very nascent. Currently, I try to teach Lean to students and am working on developing and expanding the Linear Algebra component of Lean’s math library.

Learning to Lean

Lean is accessible to anyone who understands basic theorem proving in math. You can find several learning resources here. I personally learned Lean by playing the Natural numbers game, reading Theorem Proving in Lean, and banging my head against the wall while solving some problems on Codewars.

However, online resources are not enough for learning the language as there are far too many subtle, technical difficulties that arise in practice. There are very helpful and supportive Lean communities on zulipchat and discord where you can ask questions and get help to your heart’s content.

The best way to learn is by teaching. As such, I taught a course Lean at MC2020 at Mathcamp 2020 where we taught Lean the proof of irrationality of \(\sqrt{2}\).
